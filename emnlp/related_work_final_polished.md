# Related Work — Polished Draft (2025–2026 Focus)

Recent work on LLM unlearning has moved well beyond the early question of whether a post-hoc update can suppress a target answer at all. The most important 2025–2026 papers instead ask four harder questions: whether forgetting survives future model adaptation, whether unlearning can be localized rather than globally destructive, whether current evaluation protocols actually measure true forgetting, and whether forgetting extends to modern language settings such as reasoning traces and multilingual transfer.

A first line of work studies **robustness to relearning and downstream fine-tuning**. The ICML 2025 sharpness-aware unlearning paper argues that a model has not truly unlearned if the removed knowledge rapidly reappears after subsequent tuning. The ICML 2025 invariance-based approach strengthens this point by seeking forgetting solutions that remain stable under unseen downstream fine-tuning environments, rather than only under a single known recovery attack. UAM adds an important optimization-level perspective here: rather than treating forgetting and utility preservation as separate concerns, it formulates approximate unlearning as a retain-aware optimization problem. Taken together, these papers show that modern unlearning should be judged not only by immediate post-update forgetting, but also by whether that forgetting persists after later adaptation.

A second line of work focuses on **localization and selective unlearning**. These papers start from the observation that broad global updates are often unnecessarily destructive. Adaptive Localization of Knowledge Negation for Continual LLM Unlearning argues that repeated deletion requests require concentrated, localized updates if model utility is to survive over time. On the NLP side, *Not Every Token Needs Forgetting* shows that document-level forgetting is frequently too coarse because only a subset of tokens are directly responsible for the target information. UIPE extends the same intuition by arguing that exact-target forgetting is insufficient when related knowledge can reconstruct the target. Collectively, these papers shift the field from a blunt “forget the whole example” view toward a more realistic objective: identify which parts of the model or input are truly responsible for the unwanted knowledge and intervene there while disturbing the rest of the model as little as possible.

A third and increasingly important theme concerns **evaluation faithfulness**. Several recent papers argue that standard unlearning metrics substantially overestimate progress. The EMNLP 2025 probabilistic perspective paper shows that deterministic one-shot outputs can hide the fact that the model still assigns substantial probability mass to forgotten content, meaning the target knowledge remains latent even when sampled responses look safe. The NeurIPS 2025 paper *Do LLMs Really Forget?* deepens this critique by emphasizing knowledge correlation and confidence awareness: models may preserve supporting facts or behave as though they still “know” the answer even when direct prompting no longer elicits it. These papers imply that unlearning failures are not only methodological but also evaluative. If the metric cannot capture latent retention, correlated knowledge, or confidence-based leakage, it may reward superficial forgetting rather than genuine removal.

A fourth recent direction extends unlearning into **reasoning and multilingual settings**, both of which are especially important for NLP venues. *Reasoning Model Unlearning* shows that suppressing the final answer is insufficient in reasoning-capable models because the target may still leak through intermediate reasoning traces. The ICLR 2026 paper *Explainable LLM Unlearning through Reasoning* further signals that the field is moving toward explanation-aware and reasoning-aware notions of forgetting. Meanwhile, *Learn and Unlearn* demonstrates that multilingual models can retain or even reinforce misinformation across languages, so successful English-side forgetting does not imply successful multilingual forgetting. Together, these papers argue that answer-level, English-only evaluation is no longer a sufficient target for modern LLM unlearning.

Our intended direction fits at the intersection of these four themes. The latest literature strongly suggests that there is still a meaningful gap for work that combines **selective/localized intervention** with **evaluation-faithful testing** in richer language settings such as reasoning, multi-turn recovery, and multilingual transfer. Robustness papers show that forgetting must survive future adaptation; localization papers show that broad interventions are too blunt; evaluation papers show that current metrics are too weak; and reasoning/multilingual papers show that answer-level forgetting does not fully characterize what it means to unlearn in modern LLMs. This leaves a clear opportunity for an EMNLP project that asks whether selective unlearning truly holds under language-centric recovery settings while preserving general utility.
