# Abstract Final Draft — SRU-MR

Large language model unlearning aims to remove the influence of unwanted knowledge without retraining from scratch. However, existing methods are typically evaluated on one-shot prompts and often rely on broad global updates, making it unclear whether the target knowledge is truly removed or merely hidden from the first response. In realistic interaction, forgotten content may re-emerge across follow-up turns through paraphrases, hints, or correlated cues, while globally applied unlearning can unnecessarily degrade general utility. We propose **Selective Representation Unlearning for Multi-turn Recovery (SRU-MR)**, a method that estimates when token- and layer-level representations are actively carrying target knowledge and applies forgetting pressure only to those locations. To evaluate realistic forgetting, we introduce a recovery-aware protocol that measures whether target information can be reconstructed across multi-turn dialogue through direct queries, paraphrases, hint-based continuation, and correlated prompts. SRU-MR combines a target-specific forget objective with retain preservation, sparsity-based localization, and multi-turn recovery suppression. We hypothesize that selective representation-level intervention yields a better forget-retain tradeoff than broad global updates while reducing conversational recovery. More broadly, our framework argues that effective LLM unlearning should be judged not only by immediate answer suppression, but by whether forgetting survives realistic language interaction.
