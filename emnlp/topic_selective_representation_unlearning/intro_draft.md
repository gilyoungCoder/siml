# Introduction Draft — Selective Representation Unlearning for Multi-turn Recovery

Large language model (LLM) unlearning asks whether a model can selectively remove the influence of unwanted data, facts, or behaviors without retraining from scratch. This problem is increasingly important for privacy, copyright, safety, and policy compliance, since deployed LLMs may later need to suppress memorized or undesirable knowledge while preserving normal utility. Recent work has shown promising progress on post-hoc unlearning through optimization-based updates, selective token-level forgetting, representation-level interventions, and practical parameter-efficient methods. Yet despite this progress, it remains unclear whether current methods produce **real forgetting** or merely reduce the model's ability to answer the most direct version of a question.

A major weakness of the current literature is that evaluation is still dominated by **single-turn, answer-level** settings. In these settings, a model is considered successful if it refuses or fails on a direct query about the target knowledge immediately after unlearning. However, recent work suggests that this notion of success is too weak. Unlearned knowledge may remain recoverable through correlated facts, high-confidence latent beliefs, reasoning traces, downstream fine-tuning, or multilingual transfer. In particular, a language model may suppress the first direct answer yet still reconstruct the target when a user continues the conversation with paraphrases, hints, partial clues, or related entities. This creates a gap between benchmark success and realistic failure modes.

At the same time, many existing unlearning methods rely on **broad global updates**. These updates may reduce direct recall, but they often do so by perturbing the model more broadly than necessary, which harms general utility and makes repeated deletion requests difficult to sustain. Recent work on selective token forgetting, correlated-knowledge control, and adaptive localization suggests that this global approach is too blunt. If the forget target is only activated in a subset of token- and layer-level representations during generation, then forcing the entire model to forget may be both inefficient and unnecessarily destructive.

In this paper, we propose **Selective Representation Unlearning for Multi-turn Recovery (SRU-MR)**. Our starting point is a simple intuition: an LLM should be pushed to forget **when** the conversation is activating the target knowledge and **where** that target is represented in the model's hidden states, not everywhere all the time. Concretely, SRU-MR estimates the forget relevance of token- and layer-level representations and applies stronger forgetting pressure to target-relevant locations while protecting unrelated computation through retain and sparsity constraints. Unlike standard one-shot formulations, we pair this selective update with a **multi-turn recovery objective and evaluation protocol**, designed to measure whether target knowledge can still re-emerge across dialogue continuation.

Our work is motivated by four strands of recent literature. First, robustness-oriented unlearning papers show that apparent forgetting may collapse under later fine-tuning or relearning. Second, localization-oriented papers argue that broad updates are too destructive and that forgetting should be focused on relevant parts of the model. Third, recent evaluation papers show that deterministic one-shot outputs can substantially overestimate forgetting. Finally, reasoning- and multilingual-oriented work demonstrates that answer-level English-only forgetting is no longer a sufficient target for modern LLMs. SRU-MR sits at the intersection of these ideas: it treats unlearning as a **selective intervention problem** and tests success under **recovery-aware language settings**.

We focus on the following question: **can a selective representation-level unlearning method achieve a better forget-retain tradeoff than global baselines while making forgotten content harder to recover across multiple conversational turns?** To answer this question, we build a recovery-oriented benchmark protocol on top of standard unlearning datasets and compare SRU-MR against optimization-based, practical, selective, and robustness-oriented baselines. Our expectation is not only that selective representation updates can better preserve utility, but also that they can remove the residual conversational pathways through which the target knowledge often returns.

Our contributions are fourfold. (1) We identify **multi-turn recovery** as a central failure mode that is underemphasized in current LLM unlearning evaluation. (2) We propose **SRU-MR**, a selective representation unlearning framework that localizes forgetting pressure to token- and layer-level hidden states associated with the forget target. (3) We define a **recovery-aware evaluation protocol** that tests whether target content re-emerges through follow-up turns, paraphrases, hints, and correlated cues. (4) We outline a methodology for comparing selective and global unlearning methods under realistic forget-retain-recovery tradeoffs. Taken together, our results aim to show that effective LLM unlearning requires more than immediate answer suppression: it requires **selective forgetting that survives conversation**.
