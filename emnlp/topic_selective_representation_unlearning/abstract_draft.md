# Abstract Draft — Selective Representation Unlearning for Multi-turn Recovery

## Version 1
Large language model unlearning aims to remove the influence of unwanted knowledge without retraining from scratch. However, existing methods are typically evaluated on one-shot prompts and often rely on broad parameter updates, making it unclear whether the target knowledge is truly removed or merely hidden from the first response. In practice, forgotten content may re-emerge across follow-up turns, paraphrased cues, or indirect conversational scaffolding, while globally applied unlearning can unnecessarily degrade general utility. We propose **Selective Representation Unlearning for Multi-turn Recovery (SRU-MR)**, a method that estimates when token- and layer-level representations are actively carrying target knowledge and applies forgetting pressure only to those locations. To test realistic forgetting, we introduce a recovery-oriented evaluation protocol that measures whether target information can be reconstructed across multi-turn dialogue through direct queries, paraphrases, hints, and correlated cues. Across controlled unlearning benchmarks and recovery-augmented dialogue settings, SRU-MR is designed to improve the forget-retain tradeoff relative to global baselines while reducing multi-turn recovery. Our findings suggest that effective LLM unlearning requires both **selective intervention** and **recovery-aware evaluation**, rather than one-shot answer suppression alone.

## Version 2 — slightly more EMNLP-style
Post-hoc unlearning in large language models is usually measured on single-turn prompts, yet real users can often recover the target knowledge through dialogue continuation, paraphrases, or indirect cues. This creates a mismatch between benchmark success and realistic forgetting. We address this problem with **Selective Representation Unlearning for Multi-turn Recovery (SRU-MR)**, a method that localizes forgetting pressure to token- and layer-level representations most associated with the forget target, instead of applying broad global updates. We pair this method with a recovery-aware evaluation protocol that tests whether target content re-emerges across multi-turn conversations, correlated prompts, and optional reasoning or multilingual extensions. The proposed framing is motivated by recent work showing that broad unlearning updates damage utility, while answer-level evaluation underestimates residual leakage. SRU-MR is designed to better preserve general capability while making forgotten knowledge harder to reconstruct over successive turns. More broadly, this work argues that language-centric unlearning should be evaluated not only by immediate answer suppression, but by whether forgetting survives realistic conversational recovery.
