# Related Work Draft (Latest 2025-2026 Focus)

Recent work on large language model unlearning has moved beyond the early question of whether a post-hoc update can suppress a target answer at all. The strongest 2025-2026 papers instead focus on four increasingly central issues: whether forgetting survives later adaptation, whether unlearning can be localized rather than globally destructive, whether current evaluation protocols actually measure true forgetting, and whether forgetting extends to newer language settings such as reasoning traces and multilingual transfer.

A first line of work studies **robustness and relearning resistance**. These papers start from the observation that a model may appear to forget immediately after unlearning, yet recover the target knowledge after subsequent fine-tuning or other downstream updates. The ICML 2025 sharpness-aware unlearning paper reframes forgetting as a robustness problem, arguing that a brittle forgetting solution is not sufficient if later training can quickly reactivate the removed knowledge. The ICML 2025 invariance-based work pushes this further by seeking forgetting solutions that remain stable under previously unseen downstream fine-tuning environments. UAM adds an important optimization-level perspective here: rather than optimizing forgetting alone, it treats approximate unlearning as a retain-aware optimization problem, making the forget/retain tradeoff explicit instead of leaving it as an accidental by-product of the training objective. Together, these papers suggest that strong future work must evaluate not only immediate forgetting but also whether that forgetting persists under later model adaptation.

A second line of work focuses on **localization and selective unlearning**. These papers are motivated by the empirical weakness of broad, global updates: if the entire model is pushed to forget, utility degrades quickly, especially under repeated deletion requests. Adaptive Localization of Knowledge Negation for Continual LLM Unlearning is especially important because it argues for concentrating the unlearning intervention on the parts of the model most related to the target knowledge. On the NLP side, Not Every Token Needs Forgetting makes the same point from a token-selection perspective, showing that forgetting every token in a forget document is often unnecessarily destructive. UIPE strengthens this theme by noting that exact-target forgetting is often insufficient because related knowledge can reconstruct the target, implying that selective forgetting must also reason about correlated knowledge rather than only the literal deletion string. These papers collectively move the field from coarse global suppression toward a more realistic question: how can we forget what matters while leaving unrelated knowledge and utility intact?

A third and increasingly important theme concerns **evaluation faithfulness**. Several recent papers argue that standard unlearning evaluation substantially overestimates progress. The fully probabilistic EMNLP 2025 paper shows that one-shot deterministic responses can hide the fact that the model still assigns substantial probability mass to forgotten content, meaning that the knowledge remains latent in the model even if a sampled answer looks safe. The NeurIPS 2025 paper Do LLMs Really Forget? extends this critique by focusing on knowledge correlation and confidence awareness, showing that models may retain nearby or supporting knowledge and continue behaving as though they “know” the target even when direct prompting appears unsuccessful. In this view, the field's problem is not only weak methods but also weak measurement. These papers matter because they imply that any new unlearning paper must justify its evaluation protocol much more carefully than older benchmark-centered work did.

A fourth recent direction extends unlearning into **reasoning and multilingual settings**, both of which are especially relevant for NLP venues. Reasoning Model Unlearning shows that suppressing the final answer is not enough for reasoning-capable models, since the target may still leak through intermediate reasoning traces. This paper is especially important because it shifts the unit of forgetting from answers alone to the path by which answers are produced. Explainable LLM Unlearning through Reasoning, an ICLR 2026 paper, further indicates that the field is moving toward reasoning-aware and explanation-aware notions of forgetting. Meanwhile, Learn and Unlearn demonstrates that multilingual language models can retain or even reinforce misinformation across languages, so successful English-side forgetting does not guarantee successful multilingual forgetting. Together, these papers suggest that future unlearning work should increasingly be judged in richer linguistic settings: multi-step reasoning, cross-lingual transfer, and potentially multi-turn dialogue.

Our likely direction fits most naturally at the intersection of these four themes. The recent literature strongly suggests that there is still a gap for work that combines **selective/localized intervention** with **evaluation-faithful testing** in settings that better reflect modern language use, especially reasoning and multilingual recovery. Robustness papers show that forgetting must survive future adaptation; localization papers show that broad interventions are too blunt; evaluation papers show that standard metrics are too weak; and reasoning/multilingual papers show that answer-level English-only forgetting is no longer a sufficient target. A promising contribution, therefore, is not simply another forget-loss variant, but a language-centric unlearning framework or evaluation protocol that tests whether selective forgetting truly holds under multi-turn, reasoning, or multilingual recovery while preserving general utility.
