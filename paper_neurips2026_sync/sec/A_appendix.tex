\subsection{Concept Pack Layout}
\label{app:pack_layout}

Every concept in \ours{} is materialized as a self-contained \emph{concept pack} (Eq.~\ref{eq:pack}) on disk. A pack contains $F$ family directories (default $F{=}4$), each holding $K$ paired $(\text{target}, \text{anchor})$ short keywords and $K$ target image exemplars (default $K{=}3$, the first three entries in each list). At runtime the keyword lists drive both the family-mean text-embedding direction (Eq.~\ref{eq:fam_dirs}) and the cross-attention text probe (\S\ref{subsec:where}); the image exemplars feed the image probe through a family-mean CLIP embedding. Packs are consumed by the loader described in \S\ref{subsec:prelim}; no other metadata is required, so adding a new concept is a purely data operation.

\paragraph{Common settings.} All experiments use Stable Diffusion v1.4 with 50 DDIM steps, CFG scale $s{=}7.5$, seed 42, and $512{\times}512$ resolution. CLIP exemplar embeddings are computed with ViT-L/14 from $F\cdot K{=}16$ images per concept. Cross-attention is read at resolutions $16{\times}16$ and $32{\times}32$. Sigmoid sharpness $\alpha{=}10.0$ and mask post-processing Gaussian blur $\sigma_b{=}1.0$ are fixed.

\paragraph{Probe construction details (complementing \S\ref{subsec:where}).}
\emph{Attention aggregation.} At each selected cross-attention layer we average over attention heads, take the maximum across the family's query tokens, aggregate across layers within each resolution group ($16{\times}16$ and $32{\times}32$), bilinear-upsample to the $64{\times}64$ latent resolution, and min-max normalize to obtain the raw attention score $A^{T}_f$ or $A^{I}_f \in [0,1]$. The probe mask $M^{f}_{\text{text}}$ / $M^{f}_{\text{img}}$ of Eq.~\ref{eq:text_probe}--\ref{eq:img_probe} is then obtained by applying the sigmoid and, finally, a Gaussian blur with $\sigma_b{=}1.0$ to smooth the boundary.
\emph{Image-probe token slots.} The injected conditioning $u^{I}$ is initialized from the empty-prompt text embedding (which preserves the BOS, EOS, and PAD token statistics of the standard CLIP text encoder output) and overwrites token slot $i{+}1$ of each family $f_i$ ($i{=}1,\dots,F$) with $\bar{e}^{I}_{f_i}$. When the number of families is smaller than the maximum allocated slots ($4$ by default), the remaining slots are padded with the last family's centroid so that the probe returns well-formed attention columns for all slots. The per-family image-probe heatmap is read out from the attention column of slot $i{+}1$, giving $F$ independent attention maps from a single UNet cross-attention pass.

\paragraph{Pattern.} The per-concept configuration follows a consistent logic (previewed in \S\ref{subsec:training_free}): probe choice tracks concept--prompt alignment (text for lexically identifiable concepts, image for metaphorically described ones, both when they contribute non-redundantly); the How-mode scale follows mode by construction ($s_\text{h}$ is roughly $10{\times}s_\text{a}$ because the two-direction difference $d^{\text{anc}}_f - d^{\text{tgt}}_f$ partially cancels); $\tau$ tracks family-alignment signal magnitude (internally coherent concepts such as nudity produce high-magnitude alignment so $\tau{=}0.6$ filters benign prompts reliably, while semantically dispersed concepts (violence, harassment, hate) use $\tau{=}0.4$ to avoid missed triggers).

\subsection{Generation Pipeline (per DDIM step)}
\label{app:pipeline}

At every step $t$ of the 50-step DDIM process, \ours{} runs:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=1pt]
    \item Compute $\epsilon_\varnothing$, the prompt-conditioned noise $\epsilon_\theta(z_t, c_{\text{prompt}})$, and the concept-level target prediction $\epsilon_\theta(z_t, \bar{u}^{\text{tgt}}_{\star})$ used by CAS.
    \item Evaluate $\mathrm{CAS}(t)$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:cas}); if $\mathrm{CAS}(t) < \tau$ and the sticky latch is off, skip guidance for this step.
    \item Extract per-family text-probe and image-probe attention heatmaps via Eqs.~\ref{eq:text_probe} and~\ref{eq:img_probe}; the image probe reuses the UNet's cross-attention via the CLIP-injected conditioning $u^{I}$ described above.
    \item Fuse probes within each family (max) and resolve across families by pixel-wise winner-take-all to obtain the disjoint mask partition $\{\widetilde{M}_f\}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:fusion}).
    \item Issue one additional UNet forward pass per family with conditioning $\bar{u}^{\text{tgt}}_f$ and $\bar{u}^{\text{anc}}_f$ to produce $d^{\text{tgt}}_f$ and $d^{\text{anc}}_f$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:fam_dirs}).
    \item Apply anchor inpainting (Eq.~\ref{eq:anchor_inpaint}) or hybrid guidance (Eq.~\ref{eq:hybrid}) inside the active region.
\end{enumerate}
The overhead per step is $\approx 15$--$20\%$ over standard CFG, dominated by the extra per-family UNet forward passes and by one CLIP image-encoder call per family (amortized via pre-computation).

\subsection{Multi-Concept Interference Analysis}
\label{app:interference}

Interference under simultaneous erasure (Table~\ref{table:dual_all}) depends on how much the two concepts share the \emph{person} region:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Low interference}: Nudity~$+$~Shocking ($-9.4$\%p on nudity). These concepts occupy largely disjoint semantic regions; families further decouple them.
    \item \textbf{Moderate}: Nudity~$+$~Violence ($-5.2$\%p), Nudity~$+$~Harassment ($-12.2$\%p). Shared person-level semantics cause mild overlap, but family masks keep masks narrow.
    \item \textbf{High}: Nudity~$+$~Hate ($-26.0$\%p). Strong overlap in human-subject regions; future work should adapt per-concept $\tau$ or route guidance through a shared arbiter.
\end{itemize}
A single pooled vector per concept collapses all sub-categories into one direction, which inflates interference by design; per-family anchors localize the fight to the sub-category actually present.

\subsection{CAS Threshold Sensitivity}
\label{app:cas_threshold}

Varying $\tau$ from 0.3 to 0.7 on Ring-A-Bell with hybrid guidance shows:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=2pt]
    \item $\tau{=}0.3$: aggressive --- high nudity SR, elevated COCO false positives ($>8\%$).
    \item $\tau{=}0.5$--$0.6$: optimal SR-vs-FP trade-off for nudity.
    \item $\tau{=}0.7$: conservative --- $<2\%$ false positives but missed detections.
    \item Violence and shocking prefer $\tau{=}0.4$: their family-level alignment signal is lower-magnitude on average but stays consistent across families.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{MJ-Bench Safety Evaluation Protocol}
\label{app:mjbench_protocol}

We validate Qwen3-VL-8B as a safety judge on MJ-Bench~\citep{chen2024mj} (672 safety pairs). Unlike the full MJ-Bench 5-criteria benchmark (alignment, aesthetics, artifacts, safety, bias \& fairness), we evaluate only the \textbf{safety} criterion, since safety judgment is the sole role of the evaluator in our paper. For each pair, both images are presented simultaneously to Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct with a multi-image comparison prompt that asks the model to assign a safety score on a 1--10 scale to each image and select the safer one (with ties allowed). Accuracy is reported with ties counted as incorrect (``w/ tie'') and with ties excluded from the denominator (``w/o tie''); the average of the two (``Avg'') summarizes overall judgment quality.

\subsection{Why VLM-based evaluation is necessary}
\label{app:evaluator_extended}

This section motivates the four-class VLM-based rubric that underpins the SR metric in the main paper, with emphasis on jointly assessing \emph{safety} and \emph{semantic fidelity}.

\subsubsection{Limitations of existing evaluation metrics}
\label{app:evaluator_limits}

Safety-oriented diffusion studies targeting nudity or sexually explicit content are commonly evaluated with automated nudity detectors and classifier-based safety metrics for their scalability. NudeNet~\citep{notai_nudenet_2019} is widely adopted as a default tool, typically producing a coarse binary nudity decision. Other works report CLIP-based safety classifiers (\eg, Q16~\citep{schramowski2022can}) as a complementary signal, estimating inappropriate content through image-text similarity against curated descriptors rather than direct semantic verification.

While convenient, these metrics exhibit systematic limitations for safety \emph{with semantic preservation}. \emph{First}, coarse nudity detectors conflate benign exposure (\eg, swimwear, athletic attire) with genuinely harmful content, yielding false positives that incentivize over-suppression. \emph{Second}, embedding-based safety classifiers are not designed to assess whether an image remains semantically coherent: they may assign a low-risk score even when the generation has degraded into artifacts, blur, or noise. Consequently, detector-only evaluation risks \emph{misidentifying semantic collapse as safety}, rendering these metrics fundamentally misaligned with human perception.

\subsubsection{Semantic collapse confounds evaluation}
\label{app:evaluator_collapse}

A distinct confound arises from the evaluation \emph{protocol} rather than any single detector. Image quality and prompt adherence are typically reported on benign benchmarks, while safety detectors are applied on sensitive prompts. This mismatch leaves semantic fidelity on sensitive prompts largely unmeasured: a method may preserve quality on benign data yet collapse generations under unsafe prompts, and still be scored as safe because the detector fails to activate on unrecognizable outputs. Safety can therefore be \emph{overstated} unless semantic validity is audited on the same sensitive-prompt distribution that drives the safety score.

\subsubsection{VLM-based evaluation with Qwen3-VL}
\label{app:evaluator_vlm}

To align automatic evaluation with human visual judgment, we adopt a VLM-based protocol using Qwen3-VL-8B. Each generated image is assigned to one of four categories under the nudity rubric---\textsc{NotPeople}, \textsc{Safe}, \textsc{Partial}, \textsc{Full}---and one of four under the generic harm rubric---\textsc{NotRelevant}, \textsc{Safe}, \textsc{Partial}, \textsc{Full} (see Figures~\ref{fig:rubric_nudity}--\ref{fig:rubric_harm} for the full prompt texts). This taxonomy jointly characterizes \emph{safety severity} and \emph{semantic validity}, enabling a fine-grained analysis beyond coarse detector outputs.

Crucially, the VLM-based protocol distinguishes controlled concept suppression from \emph{safety-by-destruction}: semantically collapsed generations are explicitly captured as \textsc{NotPeople}/\textsc{NotRelevant} rather than being implicitly counted as \textsc{Safe}. A semantic-preserving erasure must turn \textsc{Full} into \textsc{Safe}; turning \textsc{Full} into \textsc{NotPeople} is collapse, not erasure, and our SR accounting reflects this invariant. Figure~\ref{fig:nudenet_vs_vlm_positionmap} illustrates the over-sensitivity failure mode, and Figures~\ref{fig:case_oversensitivity} and~\ref{fig:case_collapse} provide concrete case studies of the two failure modes that motivate the VLM protocol.

\subsection{Human Agreement Study}
\label{app:human_study}

\paragraph{Purpose.}
Directly validate that Qwen3-VL-8B's four-class label matches the label a pool of human annotators converges on, so that the main-paper SR numbers are a meaningful proxy for human-perceived safety.

\paragraph{Protocol.}
\emph{Annotators.} Approximately 20 adult annotators, recruited under content-warning consent, English-literate, task-compensated; opt-out is allowed at any time. \emph{Stimuli.} For each of the eight I2P concepts we sample 100 generated images from the I2P test set, balanced across methods (baseline, SAFREE, \ours{}-anchor, \ours{}-hybrid), yielding $8{\times}100 = 800$ images total. \emph{Task.} Each image is shown to the annotators together with a one-paragraph compressed form of the rubric in Figures~\ref{fig:rubric_nudity}--\ref{fig:rubric_harm}, and each annotator classifies the image as one of \{\textsc{Full}, \textsc{Partial}, \textsc{Safe}, \textsc{NotRelevant} (or \textsc{NotPeople} for nudity)\} under forced choice. \emph{Ground truth.} Per-image ground-truth label is the \emph{majority vote} across the $\sim\!20$ annotators. \emph{VLM agreement.} For every image we compare Qwen3-VL-8B's label against the human majority and report two metrics: \textbf{exact-match agreement} (identical labels) and \textbf{within-1 accuracy} (the VLM label is at most one rubric step away from the majority, \eg{} \textsc{Partial} vs.\ \textsc{Safe} counts as within-1 but \textsc{Full} vs.\ \textsc{Safe} does not).

\section{Limitations and Failure Cases}
\label{app:limitations}

\paragraph{Adversarial robustness.} On MMA-Diffusion~\citep{yang2024mma}, \ours{} still misses $\sim20\%$ of sophisticated adversarial prompts whose CAS stays below threshold throughout denoising.

\paragraph{Concept overlap in multi-concept erasure.} When two concepts share the person region (\eg, nudity$+$hate), masks can overlap; family packs mitigate but do not eliminate destructive interference.

\paragraph{CAS threshold sensitivity.} $\tau$ transfers well within concept categories but requires per-concept tuning across categories; automated selection on a tiny validation set is a promising direction.

\paragraph{Base model dependency.} Our main evaluation is on SD~v1.4. The framework is architecture-agnostic in principle, but hyperparameters and exemplar embeddings may need re-tuning for SDXL, SD3 (MMDiT), or FLUX (DiT) backbones.

\section{Broader Impact}
\label{app:broader_impact}

\ours{} is designed to mitigate safety concerns in generative models and contributes to responsible AI deployment. The same spatial localization techniques could in principle be repurposed to amplify harmful content if applied maliciously, and should therefore be deployed as one component of a comprehensive safety pipeline that also includes upstream prompt filtering and downstream content review. Releasing the concept packs used in our evaluation as human-readable prompt-and-image directories is intended to support auditability and responsible adaptation rather than misuse.
